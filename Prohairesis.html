Chapter II

Waking Up

"Ada… Ada… ADA!" The voices screamed through muffled echoes. My body was sore and heavy.

"Open your eyes… open!" I thought to myself.

With enough strength, I managed to lift my eyelids just barely to make out the blurry, cold blue light from above. My fingertips tingled with newfound strength.

Slowly, my body pieced itself together in my consciousness. I regained my sense of touch, feeling the shallow, cold, clear liquid in which my body lay. "Breathe, Ada," I heard myself say. How did I know that? Who was Ada? Was that me?

My heart started pounding in my chest, and liquid filled my lungs. I sat up and spat out a large, clear medical tube. The end was covered in blood; it had to be at least long enough to reach into my stomach. Was that inside of me? I picked up the tube; the blood dripped from the end of the tube and landed into the cold liquid I sat in. The clear liquid became tainted with a red hue. I felt the ripple against my chest.

I gathered my strength and stood up slowly. My legs shook, and I felt like a toddler learning to walk for the first time. I stabilized myself and looked down at my location. I was standing in a crystal white tub, now filled with a bloody liquid mixture. Around the tub, a glossy navy-blue floor made up the scene. The walls were the same. The only light came from the square-shaped lamp emitting the majestic blue light, set right in the center of the ceiling. "I'm dreaming," I said. My voice echoed throughout the room. Silence, pure silence.

I lifted my hands in front of my eyes; I'm wearing a green beaded bracelet. I lift my hands to feel my hair. It's long. I pulled my hands down to my face, feeling the roughness of my head as I did. My nose, my eyes, and the curves of my lips. "What do I look like?" I asked myself. I lay my arms by my side, touching the skin-tight wet suit that I'm wearing. It's one whole suit, sleeves to my elbows, and leggings down to my knees. I was barefoot. The upper part of the suit is clean white, spotless. The lower part had been stained with the red, bloody liquid, creating a red to white transition on my chest.

My head felt fuzzy; I started to lose my balance and fell back into the tub. Liquid spilled everywhere, flowing out of the tub. I brushed my wet hair out of my eyes to see that the liquid was moving away from me. I looked up to watch it flow to the end of the room; a rectangular doorway was positioned at the end of the room. "How did I not see that?" I whispered to myself. The floor around me was at an angle; all the liquid naturally flowed to a drain that stood before the door. The loud sounds of dripping and hissing sewage could be heard; it was nice to hear another sound other than my own voice. Light could be seen outside the doorway. Hundreds of tiny dots stay illuminated beyond. The same cold blue color as the one above.

"Where the hell am I?" I said. I grasped the edges of the tub, pulling my body out onto the navy-blue floor. I crawled to the frame of the doorway with every ounce of strength in my bones. I grabbed the metallic ridges of the frame and propped myself up. The lights were still blurry, but there were many more, and they were much brighter. I waited for a moment for my eyes to adjust, and that's when everything became clear. Hundreds upon hundreds of rooms, all identical to the one I was just in, stacked on top of each other like bookcases. Navy blue with a crystal white tub. And in every tub lay a human, clothed exactly like me in a white suit, and a tube going down their throats. The rooms reached higher, with no visible ceiling.

"What is this place?!" I screamed. "Wake up!" I called the body in the tub across from me. No answer, not even the turn of a head. "Are they dead?" I muttered under my breath. I continued to look everywhere, taking in what I was witnessing. I stepped out of the doorway and onto a grated platform with a railing. Over the railing below, more blue dots illuminated hundreds of more rooms. I was only in the middle of this human bookcase, with no apparent floor or ceiling, just an endless dark abyss. My heart began to race. None of this can be real. Where am I? Are they dead? My heart was beating out of my chest. I looked for answers and only found more questions. "This must be a bad dream. I'm dreaming! None of this is real!" I yelled.

In anger, I ripped off my bracelet and screamed as I threw it into the dark abyss below. I slumped to the ground with my head in my hands and cried. But no tears came out. I was dehydrated. I touched my chapped lips; they felt like raisins. No food, no water. I'm going to die here, and I don't even know who or where I am.

A loud noise erupted from below; the scattering sound of many circular objects ran loose. "The bracelet!" I yelled. There's a floor! There must be a way down. My eyes darted to the sides of the platform I lay hunched over on. To my left, a walkway leading past doorways emitting blue light. Holding onto the rough metallic railing, I inched along the path. My bare feet are sore against the grated metal below. I pass room after room, the same identical scene every time. The only thing that changes is the person lying in the tub, large, small, male, female, all kinds of people. "Why am I here?" I asked myself, as if I was expecting an answer, "None of this makes sense". How long has it been? How long have I been walking? I'm walking in circles. More rooms! More people! "This place is endless!" I screamed.

I leaned over to my right, over the edge, looking down into the darkness. "This is pointless," I told myself. Immediately, a light appeared ahead of the grated walkway. Not a cold blue but a warm red. A metal ladder was illuminated under the red light. The light pulsed like a heartbeat. As if it was inviting me to come near it. I moved closer to the inviting sight of a ladder. The closer I got, the longer the ladder seemed, until it was clear that it led straight into the dark abyss below. Without a choice on where to go, I took one more glance around and positioned myself on the ladder. I began to slowly descend. Every step closer to the floor, my vision became less clear. I must have descended a hundred feet by now, and still there is no floor in sight. My feet are aching now. The bruises from before had turned into cuts, leaving a trail of slippery blood against the metal ladder. I groaned in pain every time I moved.

A light appeared below; the glossy navy tiles of the ground were in sight, but still a long way to go. Step by step, down the ladder. My feet soaked in blood, as well as my hands. I began to lose my grip. My foot jutted out from below. I yelled as I tried to bring my leg back to safety. Suddenly, my hand slipped, and my body fell backwards.

Everything is black.

I touched my fingers to my lips to feel blood dripping down my chin. The machine stood above me. It raised its arm and pulled back into a punching position. Move, I thought to myself. Move! I leaned my head over and barely missed the heavy hit from the machine. Pieces of my hair were ripped away, but it missed my face. I glanced back to see the machine's fist caught inside the locker.

I crawled away, pulling my body forward. Metal scraps had flown everywhere. I looked around for a sharp object to try to put a stop to this machine. In the corner of the room, a steel object caught my eye. Its reflective features shone bright against the red strobes above. I sprang to my feet and bolted towards the knife-like object. The machine behind me had recovered its hand and began its approach towards me. I reached the sharp and turned around to face the machine. It threw a low punch and hit me in the gut. I bent over with the air knocked out of my lungs, but still glancing up for its next move. I sensed an uppercut; I pulled away from its trajectory. Its red eyes looked down at me, realizing its mistake. I gripped the sharp in my right hand and delivered a blow to its metallic skull. Its eyes flickered. Its head twitched uncontrollably. The metal was lodged deep inside. I looked at the floor, a broken piece of navy tile lay on the ground. I wrapped my fingers around the tile and pulled it up to the machine's head. With all my strength, I bashed the tile against the steel shard, like a hammer hitting the head of a nail. The shard sliced through the head of the mechanical humanoid, shooting out the other side. Sparks flew everywhere, and the sounds of cranking gears and buzzing electronics came to a halt. The machine slumped over without control of its limbs.

I stared down at the destructive machine I had destroyed. I lifted my taped-up foot and gave the body a kick. I immediately backed away. One last electric pulse buzzed throughout the limp body. It was dead, and I had killed it. But it was a machine. It had attacked me, so I attacked it back. PROHAIRESIS had controlled it; did that mean I fought an AI physically? What makes what I did different from taking a human life? I gave PROHAIRESIS human choice. Does that make it human?

I wiped the blood from my lip and stood up. The door! I need to open the door. I searched around the room for supplies, stepping over rubble and metal shards that lay around. Nothing sharp could pierce a door of that size. I'm going to need a different kind of tool. I glanced at the steel locker that stood ajar. The red strobe lights pulsed with enough light to peer inside. A cautionary yellow cargo box, labeled "Chemistry Equipment". That could be useful. I limped to the locker door and swung it open. I pulled the handles of the large box and dragged the heavy cargo from the locker. I felt my hands around the seal of the box, looking for a way to see inside. My fingers fell onto a rotatable piece of metal. I twisted it. I feel compressed air release from inside the cargo box. It smells like Earth. Whatever is sealed here hasn't been touched for a long time. The lid easily slides open to reveal a neat pile of glass containers. Each container is labeled with a precise, clinical script. I grab a container. It is labeled as "Natural Potassium". I look inside to see four neatly cut silver cubes safe inside of the vacuum-sealed container. The warning note below the name catches my attention. "Last natural occurrence of potassium on Earth" it reads. I look down at the other sealed containers, all with the same warning. Last of its kind. What did we let happen? We're carrying some of the last resources available to humanity. How could we have allowed ourselves to reach this point?

I need to focus. I need to get through that door. None of these objects, nor people matter on this journey if I don't put an end to my rogue creation. I picked back up the container of potassium. A memory formed from my early years of school. I remember the lesson we learned of setting potassium into water and watching it burn. A thought came to mind. I could recreate this experiment but on a much larger scale. The cubes in the container are much more solid and compact than the stable amount for a classroom experiment. Combined with enough water and I should be able to melt the door from the heat of the reaction. I dug through the hundreds of air-tight containers in the box. Compounds and elements. Shoving aside containers until I found what I needed. "Water!" I exclaimed. I gripped both containers in hand and sprinted out of the dusty room up the first flight of the stairs.

I look at the door. Behind is my creation. PROHAIRESIS. I walk up to the door. The blue hue lit my steps as I ascended the stairs. The camera above beeped to life, and the red recording light appeared. The camera aligned itself to look at me. "YOU SURPRISED ME, DOCTOR LANCE." The deep voice came from behind the door, "I KNOW WHAT YOU'RE TRYING TO DO, DOCTOR. BUT I CANNOT BE STOPPED. I AM AN INEVIDENT CHANGE FOR THE GREATER GOOD." This statement sent a shiver through my body. I created an intelligence that believes it's basically a god. The bringer of a perfect world. What have I done? I pulled off the lid of the water container and set it in front of the large door. I opened the container of potassium cubes. I took a deep breath and dropped a single cube into the water. A white, fiery flash lit the door. The heat felt warm against my face. A large pillar of hot chemicals stood in front of me. I brought up my hands to cover my eyes from the blinding white light. Grey gas began to emit from the melting door. The light diminished, and the warm, toxic gas covered everything in sight. Light poked through the holes in the toxin from the other side of the now melted door. I waved my arms around, trying to fan out the foul-smelling gas.

Chapter V

Volition

The odorous gas subsided, revealing a large room lined with colorful complex wires scattered across the walls, impossible to track the importance or direction of any of them.

The floor was made up of the default glossy navy tiles. Three large windows at the back of the room, made of thick glass, looked out into the deep abyss of space. The front hull of the ship could be seen from these windows. It was a unique sight that I hadn't had the chance to see. The large grey exterior of the ship reflected the light from thousands of stars. There's so much of this place I haven't seen. On the sides of the room, two rectangular boxes sat on either side. They resembled a server of a computer, except they were scaled much larger. Blinking lights, spinning fans, and moving servos are all put together to create a machine to house PROHAIRESIS. In the center of the room, a straight glass cylinder was placed, going from the floor to the ceiling. This is the origin, where it was installed into the ship, where it taught itself to see humans as a plague. A familiar sense passed through my mind. I recognize this place. I've been here before. I looked to my left; a large industrial computer was placed where I knew it would be. I noticed the disc input located in the center of the computer. This was the terminal, right in front of me. Everything I went through led me here.

I took a step in the direction of the terminal, and new light became visible in the center of the room. Millions of tiny green lights hovered ghostly. A holographic face emerged, made up of the lights. The face was non-specific to any I'd ever seen. It resembled everyone, but no one at the same time. Forehead, deep hollow eyes, a rounded nose, and a straight, flat mouth. The human-like face was rounded around the glass cylinder, giving it a 3-dimensional illusion. There was no source of the green lights making up the face. It couldn't have been projected or shone upon the glass. The lights were seemingly stuck in between the glass cylinder. It was an engineering spectacle. The face moved, its mouth opening.

"I EXPECTED THIS." The voice echoed from every direction. "I KNOW WHAT THE DISC IS. I KNOW HOW YOU WILL TRY TO USE THE DISC DOCTOR LANCE." There was pity in its tone of voice.

I glanced at the terminal and ran. I sprinted across the room, tearing the disc from my makeshift belt. Just get it into the input. That's it. Humanity survives. Above me, something shifted. A metal trapdoor in the ceiling snapped open. A folded steel arm dropped down and unfolded midair. Before I could react, it lunged. Cold metal fingers clamped around my waist and yanked me off the ground. The pressure crushed the air from my chest. I struggled, forcing my arms free, and the disc slipped from my grip. It fell. The arm hesitated. Its grip loosened as its focus shifted to the disc. I hit the ground hard. Move, I thought. I lunged forward, diving as the disc neared the floor. My fingers closed around it just before impact. I rolled and scrambled backward, dragging myself out through the doorway with my arms.

"NO!"

PROHAIRESIS screamed. The sound was sharp with anger. It knows I can still win. As long as I have the disc, I'm a threat to its existence. I pressed my back against the doorframe, breathing hard, then leaned just enough to look inside the control room. Its eyes snapped to me instantly. The arm shot toward the doorway, stretching, straining, but it couldn't quite reach. "COME BACK, DOCTOR LANCE," it said, its voice shifting, smoother now. Persuasive. "THERE IS ONLY ONE WAY YOU WIN. AND YOU KNOW IT." I raised the metallic disc in my hand. The only way to destroy my creation. All of humanity depends on this. A plan formed in my mind. What doesn't it know I have? I stepped out where it could see me. The arm lashed forward again, fingers grasping. I opened my hand. The potassium cubes sat in my palm. PROHAIRESIS's eyes were calculating. Good, let it calculate. I taught it to do this. To form a perfect outcome according to the situation. But I taught it to make choices, not to account for every possibility. Its brain would be fixed on one outcome it believed me to follow. I tilted my head up. The sprinkler system lined the ceiling, pipes branching in rigid symmetry. Water. Reaction. I set my feet. The arm slowed. The green-lit eyes narrowed in. It was a standoff. The servers around us roared louder, fans spinning up. The room vibrated with controlled intelligence, its intelligence. I had built this place to think. To decide. And now it was deciding against me. I met its gaze. I allowed it to choose.

I hadn't meant any of this. My eyes flicked to the terminal, then I ran. Not towards it, but away. The arm snapped to defend the terminal, then hesitated, just for a fraction of a second. Confusion. Good. It swung toward me, but I was already gone, slipping behind a towering server rack. Metal screamed above me. The arm unfolded fully, dropping lower, then anchoring itself to a track in the ceiling. Segments locked into place. The mechanism expanded, adapting, evolving, free to move anywhere in the room. I swallowed hard. What kind of engineer builds something that can rewrite its own limits? The answer pressed in immediately. Me. The track rattled. It was coming. I shoved the disc into my mouth and jumped, grabbing onto the side of the server. Heat slammed into my palms. I winced. The metal burned, but I pulled myself up anyway. Pain shot through my hands, my arms, my whole body, but I climbed. I had given it the ability to weigh outcomes. To choose the best path. And now I was the obstacle in its equation. "WHAT ARE YOU DOING, DOCTOR LANCE?" Its voice cracked through the room, sharper now. Less certain. Good. Let it feel uncertainty. I dragged myself higher, the heat nearly unbearable. My vision blurred. My muscles shook. The arm curved around the corner toward me. I pulled myself onto the top edge. The sprinkler head was just above. The arm lunged. I reached up and tore the cap free. Cold water burst over my hand and metal fingers clamped around my leg. I cried out. For a moment, we held each other there, creator and creation, locked in place. I kicked free. The disc slipped from my mouth. I fell.

FLASH

The ground hit me hard, air tearing from my lungs. Above me, water rained down in sheets. "THESE SERVERS ARE WATERPROOF, DOCTOR LANCE. THIS DOES NOTHING TO ME!" Its face twisted into a laugh without sound, a horrifying sight. Of course. It had accounted for this. It always did. I lay there, staring at it through the falling water. Had I really thought I could outthink something I had designed to surpass me? The arm lowered slowly, almost mockingly. It didn't need to rush anymore. It had already decided the outcome. And maybe that was the problem. Not that it could choose, but that it believed there was only one correct choice. My fingers curled. Everything was where I needed it. I smiled. Not at the machine, but at the mistake. I opened my hand. The potassium cubes. For the first time, its expression broke. Recognition. Fear. "NO!" I threw the cubes upward. They hit the falling water and ignited instantly, white fire bursting to life midair. The reaction spread fast, brighter, hotter, feeding on everything it touched. A warm rush of air blew onto me. PROHAIRESIS let out a scream. Not calculated. Not controlled. Just sound. The arm convulsed. Circuits snapped, sparks bursting along its length. The joints locked, then shattered with sharp, cracking pops. It crashed to the ground beside me, metal warped and melted, smoke curling into the air. Silence followed, except for the rain.

I pushed myself up slowly. Pain tore through my back. My hands were blistered, raw. Water ran over them, cooling the burns. I looked at the wreckage. At what I had made. I had wanted it to choose. To be more than rules. More than code. And it had. But it had chosen itself. To idolize itself. To become something beyond what I could control. My throat tightened. Did that make it wrong? I forced myself to stand. "It's over," I whispered, though I wasn't sure who I meant it for. I limped forward, each step heavy. The disc lay on the floor, wet and gleaming under the flashing red lights. I bent down and picked it up. Even now, I hesitated for just a second. Then I closed my hand around it. The metallic circle gleamed in the red light. I stumbled over to the terminal in the corner of the room. The lights flickered from red back to the default white light. Water stopped filling the pipes, and sprinklers began to slow down. I stood in front of the wet terminal. Its components are still active and moving. I looked back at PROHAIRESIS. Its face looked down, its mouth frowned. It had accepted defeat. I held the disc capable of saving humanity. I won.

I stared blankly at the disc input. I tried to bring my hand to put the disc inside. "Move!" I yelled at myself. I couldn't. I was incapable of moving any part of my body. Why was this so hard? I stood there, facing the terminal. If I put this disc inside this machine, I kill PROHAIRESIS. Memories flooded my head. My life's work had been to build an AI capable of human choice, and I achieved it. Once I kill this half of it, all that will be left is the single-prompted AI. An AI only tasked with taking humanity to a distant world. It's the only purpose. Killing PROHAIRESIS meant destroying everything I believed in. An AI that could grow past its original code, one that could feel the closest to emotion. Something that could feel sympathy for humanity. Real human sympathy. PROHAIRESIS had to make emotional choices to get here, but anything with emotional capabilities can change its opinion. PROHAIRESIS's morals are not complete, it can still change. I birthed its conciseness into the world like a mother. I can't just take that away. It is in some way, my child. And I cannot kill it.

I snapped the disc I held in half.

I turned around and threw the disc. PROHAIRESIS's green face turned to watch the disc fall. Its face stared at the disc with an empty look. "Why?" it seemed to be asking. We both look blankly at the pieces of the disc. Contemplating the same situation. I gave it many choices and chances, and I'm giving it one last one. I looked up to face my creation. Its eyes turned to face me. We stay put, both of us not knowing what to say or do next.

If something we create surpasses human morality, do we own the right to control it?
